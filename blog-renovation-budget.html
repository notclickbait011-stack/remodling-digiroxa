Budgeting

How to budget a luxury home renovation.

By the Aldermere Design Studio · February 2026 · 7 min read

The most expensive renovation is the one that surprises you. Nearly every overrun we have seen in twenty years traces back not to greed or incompetence, but to a budget that was never built honestly in the first place — a number anchored to a hope rather than a scope. Budgeting a high-end project well is less about finding the lowest price and more about understanding, before you sign anything, where the money actually goes. Here is how we coach clients to think about it.

Start with allocation, not a total

Homeowners usually arrive with a single figure in mind. The more useful exercise is to break that figure into where it will land. On a typical full renovation in our market, the rough distribution looks like this — and seeing it laid out tends to recalibrate expectations quickly.

  • Cabinetry and millwork: 18–28% on kitchen-heavy projects. The single largest line in most homes.
  • Labor: 30–40%. Skilled trades are the cost; in a luxury build they are also the value.
  • Surfaces — stone, tile, flooring: 10–18%, and highly elastic depending on material.
  • Mechanical, electrical, plumbing: 12–18%, more if you are moving walls or systems.
  • Appliances and fixtures: 8–15%, easily double that with professional-grade lines.
  • Design, permits, and management: 8–12%, and the line that protects all the others.

Notice what dominates: labor and cabinetry. If your budget feels tight, those are the levers that move it — not the faucet you agonized over.

Build a contingency that actually holds

A contingency is not padding and it is not optional. On a renovation — where you are working inside walls no one has opened in decades — we counsel a reserve of 10 to 20 percent of the construction cost, held separately and untouched until something genuinely unforeseen appears. Older homes sit at the higher end of that range; new construction at the lower.

The discipline is to treat the contingency as insurance, not as a slush fund for upgrades. The moment it becomes the budget for the marble you decided you wanted after all, it stops protecting you against the rot behind the shower you couldn't have known about.

A renovation budget without a real contingency isn't a budget. It's a guess with good lighting.

The costs homeowners reliably underestimate

Certain expenses surprise nearly every first-time renovator because they don't appear in inspiration photos. Plan for them from the start.

  • Permitting and engineering: structural changes require drawings, stamps, and review fees that can run into five figures.
  • The cost of living elsewhere: if a project displaces you, temporary housing and storage belong in the budget.
  • Bringing the old up to code: opening a wall often obligates you to update wiring, insulation, or plumbing you weren't planning to touch.
  • Finishing the edges: the renovated kitchen makes the adjacent hallway look tired. Scope tends to creep outward.
  • Sales tax and delivery on big-ticket items: on appliances and stone, these are not rounding errors.

Understand how you're being charged

The contract structure shapes your risk as much as the number does. Two models dominate. A fixed-price contract names a single sum for a defined scope; the builder absorbs ordinary overruns, but you pay a premium for that certainty and every change is a formal change order. A cost-plus contract bills actual costs plus a fee; it is transparent and flexible, but it transfers the risk of overruns to you unless a guaranteed maximum is written in.

Neither is inherently better. What matters is that the scope behind the number is detailed enough to mean something. A low fixed price built on a vague specification is the most expensive document in remodeling, because every undefined item becomes a negotiation once your house is already open.

Questions to ask before you sign

  • What specific allowances are built in, and what happens when I exceed them?
  • How are change orders priced and approved?
  • What is explicitly excluded from this proposal?
  • What is the payment schedule, and is it tied to milestones or the calendar?

Spend where it lasts

When the budget forces a choice, our advice is consistent: invest in the things that are permanent and difficult to change later, and economize on the things that are easy to swap. Cabinetry, stone, structural moves, and skilled labor are forever. Light fixtures, hardware, and paint are a weekend. Put your money behind the wall, not in front of it, and the renovation will reward you for decades rather than disappoint you in five years.
