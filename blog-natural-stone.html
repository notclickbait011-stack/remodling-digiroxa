Choosing natural stone: marble, quartzite & soapstone.

By the Aldermere Design Studio · January 2026 · 8 min read

No single decision in a kitchen carries more weight, literally and aesthetically, than the stone. It is the surface you touch every day, the largest unbroken plane in the room, and often the line item clients fall in love with first and understand last. We specify three natural stones more than any others — marble, quartzite, and soapstone — and the question we are asked most is simply: which one is right for me? The honest answer depends less on looks than on how you actually live. Here is how we guide that choice.

Marble: beauty that asks something of you

Nothing replicates the luminous depth of true marble. Calacatta, Carrara, Statuario — these are metamorphic limestones, and their soft, veined character is exactly why they have graced the finest rooms for centuries. They are also, unavoidably, the most demanding surface in this comparison.

Marble is calcareous, which means it reacts to acid. A splash of lemon, wine, or vinegar will etch the surface — a dulling of the polish that is permanent and entirely separate from staining. It is also relatively soft and porous, so it will scratch and absorb if left unsealed. None of this makes marble a mistake; it makes it a material with a point of view.

Marble doesn't wear out. It wears in. The question is whether you want a surface that records your life or one that resists it.

Who marble is for

  • Homeowners who genuinely embrace patina and read etch marks as character, not damage.
  • Baking stations and butler's pantries, where the cool surface is a working advantage.
  • Bathrooms and low-traffic vanities, where acids and knives rarely appear.

If the thought of a permanent ring on your new counter would keep you up at night, we will gently steer you elsewhere — or specify a honed finish, which disguises etching far better than a polish.

Quartzite: the performer that looks like marble

Quartzite is the stone we recommend most often to clients who want the drama of marble without the anxiety. It is a different material entirely — a quartz-rich sandstone metamorphosed under intense heat and pressure into one of the hardest natural surfaces available. Many quartzites mimic the soft veining of marble convincingly, yet behave far more forgivingly.

Because it is silica-based rather than calcareous, true quartzite resists acid etching and is markedly harder than marble — it shrugs off knives and most daily abuse. One caution worth knowing: the stone trade sometimes mislabels softer dolomitic marbles as quartzite. We always test a sample for etching and hardness before we let a slab carry the name, and you should insist your fabricator does the same.

Who quartzite is for

  • Busy family kitchens that see real cooking and want a near-bulletproof surface.
  • Clients drawn to the look of marble but unwilling to manage its temperament.
  • Anyone who wants natural stone, not engineered quartz, with minimal upkeep.

Soapstone: quiet, warm, and honest

Soapstone is the introvert of the three — and for the right home, the most rewarding. It is a steatite, soft to the touch and rich in talc, with a depth of color that ranges from dove gray to near-black and a subtle veining all its own. It is also chemically inert, which means acid does nothing to it; you can set a cut lemon on it without a second thought.

Its softness is the trade-off. Soapstone will dent and scratch more readily than quartzite, but those marks can be sanded out, and most owners simply let them accumulate into a working patina. Rubbing it periodically with mineral oil deepens the color and unifies its appearance over time. It is the only one of the three that asks for almost nothing chemically while quietly developing real character.

Specifying like a professional

Whichever stone you choose, the difference between an ordinary installation and an exceptional one lives in the details — and most of them are decided before fabrication begins.

  • Select the actual slab. Stone is geological, not a catalog item. Visit the yard and tag your slabs; veining and color vary dramatically piece to piece.
  • Choose your finish deliberately. Honed reads soft and hides wear; polished amplifies veining and reflects light; leathered adds tactile depth and forgives marble's etching.
  • Plan the seams. A skilled fabricator hides seams in the field's quietest area and book-matches veining across them; insist on a layout drawing.
  • Detail the edge. A thin mitered edge reads modern; a thick or shaped profile reads traditional. The edge is the stone's signature.
  • Seal what needs sealing. Marble and most quartzites benefit from a quality impregnating sealer; soapstone takes oil, not sealer.

There is no universally correct stone — only the one that matches how you live. If you cook hard and worry little, quartzite. If you want warmth and honesty and a surface that ages with grace, soapstone. And if you are willing to enter into a relationship with a material that gives back exactly what it takes, marble will reward you like nothing else can. The right slab, well specified, is a decision you will be glad of every single day.
